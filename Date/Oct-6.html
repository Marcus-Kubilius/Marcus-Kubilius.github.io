Marcus' Trip to Japan

In the Joseon dynasty, this was the original street coming away from the main palace (there were 4 other, lesser, palaces, and it was all government offices. The modern Korean government decided to move the offices away from this traditional city centre because they wanted the population to be more dispersed and enjoy life. The modern Korean government has put a lot of work into the preservation of history and has even fenced-off areas showing the ancient street drains and wells, a timeline of historical events from ancient times to the present, and statues for notable historical figures along the street. The most notable statue, the statue of Sejong, is located in front of the palace. Sejong is beloved by Koreans because he spent a lot of time improving the lives of average Koreans by inventing/introducing tools to help improve farming yield and consistency. In and around 1910, a lot of history was lost due to the Japanese colonization. During this time, a lot of history was destroyed or sent back to Japan. Regardless, the Korean government has taken great steps to preserve and display as much of the Korean heritage for future generations. There is clearly still animosity harboured towards the Japanese oppression of Korea over the centuries. Actually, on the first metro train car we were in on our way from Incheon airport, there was an ad, or perhaps a government-sponsored informational video, playing on about the Dokdo island and how the world had acknowledged that it belongs to Korea and Japanese documents, even dating back hundreds of years ago, acknowledged that the island was Korean but now they are disputing that and trying to lay claim to it.

The Cheonggyecheon stream is, and has been very important and central to many Koreans. In 1953, the Korean War ended and at that point, South Korea was the second poorest country in the world. Residence and livelihood often are centred around bodies of fresh water so many of the poorest citizens congregated around the stream and stayed there to live and try to work. Then, around 1988, Korea started changing drastically due to the Olympics. Because the Cheonggyecheon was so central but also had grown to be very dirty and a gathering place for the homeless, before the 1988 Olympics, the government covered the edges of the stream with concrete and kicked out the poor and homeless to improve their appearance to foreigners. The economy grew very fast after the Olympics and into the 90s the government found that had a lot of money and started preserving the environment. This included breaking up some of the concrete and improving the ecosystem of the stream (To this day, there are many fish in the stream largely due to the culture of not harming unnecessarily). At night many normal Korean people bring Beer, cheese, and dry squid to eat around the stream. When doing so, they often use the stream to cool beer as there is a very big beer-drinking culture in Korea.

After our journey through a bit of Korean history, we briefly visited a gallery of sorts that had an exhibit featuring some drawings in a more modern Korean style. I particularly like the one where it gives the effect of viewing the world as if through a pinhole camera with a curved film plane. I enjoy traditional straight line perspective drawings so seeing this take on the media with the curved effect was striking to me.

After the art exhibition, we grabbed lunch, see the last post for pictures, and then headed out on a bus to Inkigayo. Inkigayo is a South Korean music (KPOP) program broadcast by SBS, a leading Korean TV and radio broadcast network. Inside the broadcast studio, there was a strict no phones, no cameras policy but we were sat right next to the MC platform and very near the main performance stage. It was fascinating to watch the MCs being recorded and broadcast live from the platform in front of us as if we were backstage at a news station. Before entering the venue, we had to wait in a line for quite some time before we were slowly filtered inside after showing our tickets and passports. During the actual performances, some performers were only displayed on a digital screen, some were prerecorded but the group/artist still came out on stage to dance to their song live (as many feature KPOP songs have a choreographed dance that goes alongside the song’s release which is performed while singing), and some appeared to perform their song live.

For lunch, which was the very first sit-down restaurant we frequented on the trip, we were initially given the wrong order however we were able to get it sorted without speaking much if any Korean or English. We ordered a radish soup, dumplings, and Naengmyeon as a set. It was served with a hot broth drink and water. The pear slivers in the soup were sweet and delicious. It was an excellent complement to the soup’s flavour profile. For dinner, after Inkigayo, we got some Korean fried chicken and beer, actually in Korea this time. The chicken at this specific restaurant we went to was different from Dawa, the Korean Fried Chicken restaurant in Montreal that I love. We ordered 4 different seasonings and three of them were dry spiced as well and unexpectedly quite sweet. The fourth was more familiar and on the spicier side. I will definitely need to go to more chicken places in Korea to investigate and compare further. I know that my favourite chicken dish at Dawa is made using a traditional sauce with gochujang (a red chilli paste), honey, and sesame oil among other things so I would expect to be able to find a similar if not identical offering within Korea and I am excited to see how it compares.
