Marcus' Trip to Japan

Although the Korean War ended in 1953, there remains a real conflict between the North and South. To keep itself defensible and war-ready, South Korea has mandatory military service for all male citizens between the ages of 18 and 35. In addition, there are caches of emergency/relief supplies and emergency shelters throughout the metro network. Furthermore, they have a well-implemented emergency alert system. They are much less intrusive and annoying than Canadian “Amber Alerts” because they do not make a blaring noise when an alert comes in. Instead, a message silently pops up on the phone screen. I personally think this is much more effective as I have gotten into the habit of closing Amber Alerts very fast and not always bothering to read it as I am more focused on getting rid of the nuisance. To combine these two points a bit, one of the emergency alerts we received was a warning to be careful because the North Koreans sent another garbage balloon (which historically have been filled with propaganda leaflets, human waste, or used face masks in the past) as a form of provocative psychological and biological warfare. On a happier note, their fire safety regulations also seem great. You see fire extinguishers and emergency flashlights everywhere.

We stopped at a Konbini at the train station to grab breakfast. The train to Busan was a bit of an interesting experience, they did not check our tickets at any point from entering Seoul KTX station and exiting the Busan KTX station. They just have a list of which seats should be full and don't check tickets at all by default. Throughout our time here, from what I’ve observed, there seems to be a significant amount of trust (and also a significant amount of surveillance cameras). Shops store goods outside the storefront, owners are away from their stalls frequently, and the general approach/mindset is that of believing that people are being honest. A perfect example of this is when we asked for a second room key for our Seoul lodging. From what we could tell, the guy running the check-in counter gave us the key without any checks to verify that it was our room. Do note that we had never seen this guy before and he hadn’t even seen our room key or asked for the name our reservation was under. He asked for the room number, made the key and then we used the key he just gave us to swipe into the elevator (the floor we were staying on is key-card-protected unlike the lobby) and went on our way.

Our first stop after arriving in Busan was the Gamcheon Culture Village. It felt reminiscent of the famous Brazillian favela slums on the mountain Rocinha, outside Rio de Janeiro by the Christo Redeemer statue. The village was historically a slum to house the workers for the seaport but in 2009, the Korean government began a process to renovate it as a public art project to make it a “cultural hub.”

Afterwards, we went over to the Gukje market and bought some lunch. We got a Pajeon, Tteokbokki, and Shine Muscat (a delicious Asian grape) for dessert.

We walked around Busan for a while before grabbing dinner. We went to a local sashimi resturant and ordered a miscelanious set, a salmon set and a oxtail side dish. The salmon was amazing - very rich and flavourful considering that is an unseasoned piece of raw fish. the oxtail dish was nice too, it fairly spicy, overpowering the meat’s flavour a bit with the chili sauce but it was still enjoyable. The dish came with a stack of Shiso leaves and the fish could be wrapped in reminiscent to how sushi is wrapped in seaweed.
